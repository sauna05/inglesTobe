Simple Present Tense

What is the Simple Present Tense

The simple present is a tense in English used to express habitual actions, routines, permanent states, and general facts. It is used to talk about things that happen regularly or that are always true.

Uses of the simple present

When you want to communicate permanent states or actions.

She works as a doctor

To indicate routine states or situations, or actions that occur periodically.

I drink coffee every morning.

To talk about habits, routines, or facts that repeat over a set period of time.

They go to the gym three times a week.

When giving directions, instructions, or signals.

Turn right at the next corner.

To convey appointments, facts, or commitments that will happen at a specific time.

The meeting starts at 10 a.m.

Attention!

The simple present should not be used for actions happening at the moment of speaking. Use the present continuous instead for these situations.

Structure of the Simple Present:

To construct sentences in the simple present, it's important to distinguish between two groups of subjects:

Group 1: I, You, We, They

Structure:

Subject + Verb in normal form + Complement

Subject verb Example
I drink I drink too much water.
You go you go to the house
We dance we dance tango
They play they play soccer

Group 2: She, He, It

For this group, known as the third person singular, the following rules apply to verbs:

Add -s to all verbs

She eats chicken.

For verbs ending in -o, -sh, -ch, -ss, -x, and -z, add -es at the end.

He wishes for a new car

For verbs ending in -y, preceded by a consonant, replace -y with -i and add -es.

He spies on his neighbors.

For verbs ending in -y, when there is a vowel before the -y, keep the -y and add -s.

It enjoys playing in the park.

Warning

To be:

Has its own forms in the simple present: am, is, are.

To Have:

It has its own form for use with the third person singular (she, he, it): has.

Structure:

Subject + verb with respective rule applied+ Complement

Subject verb Example
She pays She pays the bills.
He goes He goes to school
It shines It shines brightly during the day.

Adverbs of Frequency:

In the simple present, we use several adverbs of frequency to indicate how often an action occurs. These adverbs are usually placed before the main verb.

Always - 100%

I always go to the gym

Every day - 100%

They exercise every day.

Usually - 80%/90%

She usually eats breakfast at 8 a.m.

Often - 70%/60%

He often travels for work.

Sometimes - 50%/40%

Do you sometimes forget your keys?

Rarely - 20%/10%

We rarely eat out at restaurants.

Hardly ever - 10%/5%

I hardly ever go to the theater

Never - 0%

Does she never drink soda?

Auxiliary "Do/Does":

Note:

Do not confuse with the verb "to do". "Do" and "does" do not have meanings by themselves, but help us construct questions and negative sentences in the simple present. They allow the use of the verb in its base form, without changes, even for the third person singular. As with the affirmative form, we separate the subjects into two groups to use the corresponding auxiliary DO/DOES.

DO

I, YOU, WE AND THEY

Negative form:

Subject + do + not + verb + complement

I do not (don't) drink too much water

Interrogative form:

Do + subject + verb + complement + ?

Do you drink too much water? -Yes, I do.

DOES

SHE, HE AND IT

Negative Form:

Subject + does + not + verb + complement

He does not (doesn't) drink too much water

Interrogative Form:

Does + subject + verb + complement + ?

Does he drink too much water? -No, he does not (doesn't).

Question Words With Do and Does

What - Who - Where - When - Why - How

Structure:

Question Word + Auxiliar Do/Does+ Subject + Complement + ?

What do you do on the weekends?

Who do they invite to the party?

Where does she work?

When does he start his job?

Why do you study English?

How does he feel today?
